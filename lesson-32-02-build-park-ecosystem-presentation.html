📖 The Lesson: Designing a Science Presentation
Read carefully — this sets up everything you'll build next.
⏱ 10 min
🎯
Section 1
What Makes a Good Presentation?

Scientists don't just collect data — they share it. A presentation helps other people understand what you found. But not every presentation does that well.

Good presentations are built on criteriaCriteriaThe specific requirements a design must meet to be successful.. Think of criteria like a recipe. Leave out an ingredient and the dish doesn't come out right. Leave out a criterion and your audience doesn't get the full picture.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If you wanted someone to understand the Everglades by looking at your display, what's one thing they'd absolutely have to see?"
🔍
Section 2
Evidence Is the Foundation

Knowing something and proving it are different. EvidenceEvidenceSpecific facts or observations that support a claim — not guesses or opinions. is specific — it's a fact, an observation, or a number that backs up your claimClaimA statement about what you think is true, backed up by evidence..

"Wolves live in Yellowstone" is a claim. "Gray wolves travel in packs, which lets them hunt elk that are much larger than a single wolf could tackle" — that's evidence. One is a sentence. The other teaches you something real about the ecosystem.

📋

Your ecosystem presentation has six criteriaCriteriaThe specific requirements a design must meet to be successful.. Every presentation must include all six to be complete.

⭐ Key Concept

Every presentation must meet all six criteria to be complete. Those criteria are what make it science — not just art.

You also have constraintsConstraintA limit on your design — like time, materials, or space. — limits on your time, materials, and space. Good engineers think about both: what it must do AND what it can realistically be built with.

🤯
Amazing Science Fact
The National Park Service publishes thousands of scientific reports every year. Scientists who study parks spend as much time communicating their findings as they do collecting them. Sharing science clearly is part of the job.
Curious? Go Deeper

Science communication is an actual career. Ecologists, park rangers, and field scientists often work alongside science writers and education specialists who turn complex findings into clear presentations, exhibits, and reports.

The best science communicators aren't just good at explaining — they understand the science deeply enough to decide what to leave out. Choosing what NOT to include is one of the hardest parts of designing a presentation.

Some scientists work entirely on communicating research. They design museum displays, write national park interpretive signs, and create educational materials that reach millions of visitors each year. Your presentation is the same kind of work — just smaller scale.

🥾 Design Challenge: Build Your Presentation
Follow these four steps to plan and build.
⏱ 20 min
🥾 Your Design Mission

Park rangers want visitors to understand what's happening in national park ecosystems. But most visitors only have a few minutes to stop and look. Your job is to design a presentation — a poster, display board, or set of slides — that explains your park clearly enough for any visitor to understand in about two minutes.

The Problem
Visitors want to understand an ecosystem — but scientists' reports are too hard to read.
What You're Making
A visual presentation that communicates your park's ecosystem science to a general audience.
How You Know It Works
Your presentation meets all six criteria and fits within your constraints.
📋 Your Six Presentation Criteria — At a Glance
🏞️ Park name & location
🌡️ Environmental conditions
🐾 3+ key organisms & how they survive
🕸️ Food web showing energy connections
⚠️ One ecosystem challenge
🛡️ One conservation effort

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you. See what you've got before the assignment.
1. What is a design criterion?
AA list of materials you are allowed to use
BA specific requirement your design must meet to be successful
CA description of what your design looks like
🎯 Exactly. A criterion is a requirement — something your design must do or include to be complete and successful.
A criterion is about what your design must achieve, not what it looks like or what materials you use. Think: what must my presentation DO to be considered complete?
2. Maria has 30 minutes to build and can only use paper and markers. What do scientists call those limits?
ACriteria
BEvidence
CConstraints
⏳ That's it. Time and materials are constraints — limits that affect what your design can realistically be. They're different from criteria, which describe what it must accomplish.
Criteria are what the design must do. Constraints are what limits the design — time, materials, and space. Maria's 30 minutes and markers are limits on what she can make, so those are constraints.
3. Which of these is the strongest piece of scientific evidence for an ecosystem presentation?
A"Wolves are important animals in Yellowstone."
B"Yellowstone has a lot of interesting wildlife."
C"Gray wolves travel in packs, which lets them hunt elk that are much larger than a single wolf could tackle alone."
🔍 Strong evidence is specific and detailed. It shows exactly how something works — not just that it exists. The first two options are general claims. Option C teaches you something real.
The first two are general — they don't tell you anything new or specific. Real evidence has a detail that helps you understand something. Look for the option that explains a behavior or mechanism, not just states a fact.
