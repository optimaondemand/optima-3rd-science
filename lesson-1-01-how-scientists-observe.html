📖 The Lesson: What Scientists Do
Read carefully — scientists are careful readers and careful lookers
⏱ 12 min
🌱
Section 1
What Scientists Actually Do

Before scientists build machines or discover cures, they do something simple. They look. Really carefully.

That careful looking is called an observationObservationSomething you notice using your senses — what you can actually see, hear, smell, touch, or taste.. An observation is anything you notice using your five senses. The color of a leaf. The sound of frogs. The rough texture of tree bark. These are all observations.

Scientists who study National Parks spend their days outside, filling notebooks with observations. They might notice the tracks of a black bear near a stream, or count how many pine trees are growing on a hillside. Every observation gets written down. That's the job.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Think about the last time you were outdoors. What did you notice? Were those observations or just things you saw without really thinking about them? What's the difference?"
💭
Section 2
Observations vs. Inferences

Here's where science gets interesting. An observationObservationWhat you notice using your senses — what's actually there. is what you actually notice. An inferenceInferenceA reasonable explanation or guess based on observations. It's what you think is happening. is what you think is happening based on what you noticed.

Scientists are very careful to keep these two things separate. If you mix them up, you can make mistakes.

✅ Observation 💭 Inference
The mud near the pond has deep round prints in it. A deer probably walked here this morning.
The leaf has brown edges and feels dry and crisp. This leaf probably fell from the tree a few days ago.
There are chewed marks on the bottom of the tree trunk. A beaver has been working on this tree.

See the pattern? Observations stick to what you can actually see, touch, hear, or smell. Inferences use those observations to make a reasonable explanation — but they could still be wrong. Maybe it wasn't a deer. Maybe it was a large dog. That's why scientists keep gathering more observations before they decide.

🤔
Stop and Think
"You find an empty bird nest on the ground under a tree. Write one observation about it. Then write one inference. How do you know which is which?"

Section 3
Tools, Field Notes & the Park Scientist

Scientists don't just observe with their eyes. They use toolsToolAn object that helps you observe or measure more carefully — like a ruler, magnifying glass, thermometer, or collecting jar. to observe things they couldn't notice otherwise.

🔍
Magnifying Glass
Makes tiny things look bigger. You can see veins in a leaf, tiny insects on bark, or the texture of a seed coat.
📏
Ruler
Gives an exact measurement so you can compare sizes. How long is the leaf? How wide is the track?
🌡️
Thermometer
Measures temperature. Is the water in a pond warmer in summer? A thermometer gives you the number.
📓
Field Notebook
The most important tool of all. This is where scientists record everything — sketches, measurements, and questions.

Scientists who study Everglades National Park carry field notebooks everywhere they go. When they spot a roseate spoonbill wading in the marsh, they don't just watch — they record the time, location, what the bird was doing, and a quick sketch. Those field notesField NotesWritten records of observations made outdoors. Scientists depend on them to track patterns over time. might be used years later to show whether the bird population is growing or shrinking.

⭐ Key Concept

Science begins with careful observation. An observation is something you notice using your senses. An inference is a reasonable explanation you make based on your observations. Keeping these two things separate is one of the most important habits in science.

Every scientist — from biologists in the Everglades to geologists in Yellowstone — depends on evidence (recorded observations) before drawing any conclusions.

🤯
Amazing Science Fact
Naturalist John Muir filled more than 60 notebooks with observations while exploring Yosemite in the 1800s. He wrote down the color of ice at different depths, the sound of waterfalls at different times of day, and the exact number of species on a single hillside. Those careful observations helped convince the U.S. government to protect Yosemite as a National Park in 1890 — and the parks still exist today because someone took the time to really look.
🔭 Curious? Go Deeper

Scientists who study nature in the field are often called naturalists. Naturalists spend years learning to notice things other people walk right past — a tiny spider web in morning dew, a faint scratch mark on a tree, the way certain plants only grow near water. This skill isn't magic. It comes from practicing observation over and over again.

Charles Darwin, who developed the theory of evolution, was one of the most careful observers in history. On his voyage around the world, he collected thousands of specimens and filled page after page of notebooks with descriptions. He was 22 years old when he started. The observations he made on that trip took 20 more years of thinking before he understood what they meant — but the observations came first.

You're starting the same way Darwin did. Write down what you see. Draw what you can. Keep asking questions. That's the whole foundation of science.

🔬 Observation Practice: Park Scientist
Practice the skill that every scientist starts with
⏱ 20 min
🎒

You'll need: Science notebook, pencil, colored pencils, and a natural object you can observe (a leaf, rock, seed, stick, or anything from outside). A magnifying glass is great if you have one.

Park scientists have a habit: they observe first and explain later. Right now you're going to practice exactly that. Follow each step in order.

1
Just Look — Don't Write Yet

Set your natural object in front of you. Look at it for one full minute without picking it up or writing anything. Notice as many details as you can. Turn it over in your mind even if not your hands yet.

2
Use Your Senses — Record Observations

Now pick it up (if safe) and use as many senses as you can. In your science notebook, write at least 5 observations. Each one should start with what you actually notice — color, size, shape, texture, smell, or weight.

Try starting your observations with: "It looks like…" "It feels…" "It smells like…" "It is about as long as…" "I notice…"

3
Sketch and Label

Draw your object in your notebook — it doesn't need to be perfect, just detailed. Label at least 3 parts using observations (not names). For example: "rough, bumpy surface" or "curved edge with a point at the tip."

4
Now Make Inferences

Based on your observations, write 2 inferences about your object. What do you think is true about it that you can't directly see? Where did it come from? What happened to it?

Try starting with: "I think this might be… because I noticed…"

5
Check Your Work

Look back at what you wrote. Label each note with an O for observation or an I for inference. Are you confident in your labels? If a sentence has the word "probably" or "I think" in it, it's almost certainly an inference.

💬
Record Your Thinking
Tell the Story of Your Observation

In your science notebook, write 3–4 sentences telling the story of your observation from start to finish. What did you do first? What surprised you? What did you notice that you didn't expect?

🤔
Think About This
"Was it hard to keep your observations and inferences separate? When did you catch yourself inferring instead of observing? What made you stop and fix it?"
🌿
Connect to the Parks
"If a scientist in Everglades National Park found your object on a trail, what would they write in their field notes? What inferences might they make about the ecosystem around it?"

🧠 Self-Check: Test Your Thinking
Three questions — read carefully before you answer

Question 1 of 3
A scientist writes: "The leaf is oval-shaped, dark green, and about 4 centimeters long." This is an example of —
A An inference
B An observation
C A field note guess
D A prediction
✅ Exactly right! The scientist is describing exactly what they can see — shape, color, and size. That's an observation. It uses the senses and sticks to the facts.
Not quite. Look again — the scientist is describing what they can directly see: shape, color, and size. No guessing involved. That makes it an observation, not an inference.
Question 2 of 3
A park scientist finds muddy footprints near a pond and writes: "A deer must have walked through here this morning." This is an example of —
A An observation
B A measurement
C An inference
D Field notes
✅ Yes! The scientist didn't actually see the deer. She looked at the footprints (observation) and made a reasonable explanation about what happened (inference). Good thinking!
Look carefully — the scientist didn't see the deer herself. She's using the footprints she did see to make a guess about what happened. That guess is an inference, not an observation.
Question 3 of 3
Which science tool would help a scientist see the tiny veins on the back of a small leaf?
A A thermometer
B A magnifying glass
C A ruler
D A field notebook
✅ Right! A magnifying glass makes small things appear larger, so you can observe tiny details you'd miss with just your eyes. It's one of the most useful tools a park scientist carries.
Think about which tool helps you see small things up close. A ruler measures length, a thermometer measures temperature, and a notebook records — but only one tool makes tiny things appear bigger.
