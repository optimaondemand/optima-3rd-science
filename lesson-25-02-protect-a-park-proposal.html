📖 The Lesson: What Goes Into a Proposal?
Learn the parts of a conservation proposal before you write yours.
⏱ 8 min
⚠️
Section 1
What Threatens a Habitat?

Every habitat has things that could hurt it. ThreatsThreatA danger that could harm a living thing or damage the place where it lives. can come from pollution washing into a river, invasive plants crowding out native ones, or too many visitors trampling a fragile marsh. Sometimes the threat is slow — habitat loss from roads and buildings creeping into wild land.

Scientists who study these problems notice patterns over time. They count species, measure water quality, and track which plants are disappearing. That's how they know something is wrong before it's too late.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If you walked into a park and noticed the water looked brown and the fish were gone, what would you want to know next?"
📋
Section 2
The Four Parts of a Proposal

A habitat protection proposalProposalA written plan that describes a problem and explains a solution that could fix it. has four parts. The problem — what's happening and why it matters. The evidence — the facts and observations that prove the problem is real. The solution — what you think should be done. The criteria — how you'll know if it worked.

Scientists use proposals like this when they want park rangers, government officials, or communities to take action. A proposal without evidence is just an opinion. With evidence, it becomes a case.

Five common threats that conservation scientists study and write proposals to address. Each one needs a different kind of solution.
⭐ Key Concept

When a habitat is threatened, scientists and citizens write protection proposals. A strong proposal names the problem, presents evidence, describes a solution, and explains how you'll know if it worked.

A proposal without evidence is just an opinion. Evidence turns an opinion into a case.

🤯
Amazing Nature Fact
The Florida Everglades Restoration Project is one of the largest habitat protection efforts in history — covering 18,000 square miles. Scientists spent 30 years gathering evidence before the plan was approved. Their proposals convinced the government to spend more than $10 billion to restore the water flow.
Curious? Go Deeper

Why do proposals matter so much? Scientists who discover a problem can't fix it alone — they need resources, permission, and people to do the work. A well-written proposal is how scientists communicate urgency to the people who have the power to act.

In the 1960s, a scientist named Rachel Carson wrote a book called Silent Spring about how pesticides were killing birds across the country. It wasn't just a report — it read like a story. It had evidence, vivid descriptions, and a clear argument. It changed how the entire country thought about chemical pollution and eventually led to the creation of the EPA. That's the power of making a case with evidence.

🌿 Engineering Challenge: Write Your Proposal
Identify a real threat — then design and argue for a solution.
⏱ 20 min
🎒

You'll need: Science notebook, pencil, and the Proposal Planning Chart below. Copy the chart into your notebook or use the one shown here to plan your ideas.

Park rangers and conservation scientists write habitat protection proposals all the time. Today you'll do the same thing — pick a real threat, think through what you know, and design a solution you can defend with evidence.

1

Pick Your Park Problem. Choose one threat to focus on. You can use one of the five listed below — or think of something you've learned about in this unit. Write your choice at the top of your planning chart.

🏭 Pollution 🌱 Invasive Species 🏗️ Habitat Loss 👣 Over-Visitation 🌡️ Climate Threats
2

Gather What You Know. In the "Evidence" row of your chart, write at least two things you know about this threat. What does it do to the habitat? Which plants or animals are affected? What causes it? Write specific facts — not just "it's bad."

3

Design Your Solution. In the "Solution" row, write what you'd actually do about the threat. Be specific — not just "stop the pollution," but how. Think about who would need to help, what would have to change, and what resources you might need.

4

Set Your Criteria. In the "Criteria for Success" row, describe what success would look like. How would you know the solution is working? Think of something you could measure or observe — like "fish are returning to the river" or "invasive plants are decreasing."

5

Strengthen Your Claim. Read your whole chart. Then write one sentence at the bottom that starts: "I believe this proposal would work because…" That sentence is your claim. Make it count — it's the most important part.

📓 Record Your Thinking
Organize what you discovered and defend your proposal.
⏱ 8 min

Think through your full proposal using your planning chart. A strong proposal comes from someone who really cares — and really knows what they're talking about. You'll write this up in the Assignment tab.

1 Write the Problem section. Describe what's happening in the habitat. Be specific — name the habitat, the threat, and the effect on plants or animals.
2 Write the Evidence section. List at least two pieces of evidence you used. Explain why each one shows the threat is real — not just that you noticed it, but what it means.
3 Write the Solution + Criteria section. Explain what you'd do and how you'd know it worked. Draw a quick sketch of your solution in action — label at least two parts.
🗣

Tell the whole story of your proposal out loud — from the problem, to the evidence, to the solution. If you can say it clearly without looking at your notes, you really understand it.

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See what you remember before writing your assignment.
1. What is a habitat?
AThe place where a plant or animal lives and gets the food, water, and shelter it needs
BA plan that scientists write to protect nature
CA list of rules for visitors at a national park
🌿 Yes! A habitat is the place where an organism lives — where it gets everything it needs to survive.
Think about the vocab card you flipped. A habitat is a place — not a plan and not a list of rules.
2. A student notices that pollution from a nearby road is washing into a park river and killing fish. What should their habitat protection proposal include?
AEvidence that the pollution is harming the fish, plus a specific plan to reduce runoff
BA drawing of a healthy river with no roads nearby
CA list of every type of fish that lives in the river
🌲 Exactly! A strong proposal always connects the problem to real evidence and explains a specific solution — not just what's wrong, but what to do about it.
A proposal needs both evidence AND a solution. A drawing or a species list alone doesn't explain what to do or why it would help.
3. Which of the following is the best example of a "criteria for success" in a habitat protection proposal?
A"The park has many different animals living in it"
B"We want to protect the Everglades wetlands"
C"The number of nesting birds increases within two years of the cleanup"
⭐ That's it! Criteria need to be specific and measurable. "Nesting birds increase within two years" is something you can actually check — that's what makes it useful.
Criteria for success should be specific and measurable — something you can actually observe and count. "We want to protect" is a goal, not a measure of success.

🔍 Part A — Identify and Classify

Answer each item in your science notebook using what you learned today.

1List three different threats that could damage a national park habitat. Write one sentence about each threat explaining what it does.
2For each threat you listed, write one type of evidence a scientist could use to show the threat is real. (Example: "To prove pollution is a problem, you could test the water quality every month.")
3Circle the threat from your list that you wrote your protection proposal about.

🚀 Part B — Short Answer

Answer each question in complete sentences using your science vocabulary.

4What problem did you describe in your proposal? Explain it in two complete sentences. Be specific about what is being harmed and why it matters.
5Why is evidence important in a habitat protection proposal? Give a specific example from your own work today.
6What criteria did you choose for your proposal? Explain how you would know — by observing or measuring something — that your solution is actually working.
✍️ Write and Explain

You finished your habitat protection proposal. Now explain it — and make the case for why it would work.

What threat did you choose, and what evidence tells you it's a real problem?

💡 Start with: "The threat I chose is ___, and I know it's a problem because…"

What does that evidence tell you about the habitat?

💡 Start with: "This evidence shows that…"

Why does your proposed solution make sense?

💡 Start with: "My solution would work because…"

📚 Try to use at least two of these words in your answer: habitat, threat, evidence, criteria, conservation

🎨 Part C — Draw & Label

In your science notebook, draw a simple map or diagram of your park habitat. Show: (1) where the threat is happening — label it, (2) where your solution would take place — label it, and (3) at least two plants or animals that would be helped by your plan. Label them too.
