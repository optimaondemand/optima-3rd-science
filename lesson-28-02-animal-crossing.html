📖 The Lesson: Wildlife Crossings
Read carefully — you'll use this knowledge to design your own.
⏱ 10 min
🔄
Section 1
From Problem to Solution

You already know the problem: roads cause habitat fragmentationHabitat FragmentationWhen a road or barrier splits an animal's habitat into smaller, disconnected pieces., cutting animals off from food, water, and mates. Today is about what comes next — the engineering.

Engineers don't jump straight to building. They follow a cycle: plan, build a prototypePrototypeA first test version of a design, built to see how it works before making the final thing., test it, and reviseReviseTo change a design after testing — fixing what didn't work and making it better.. That cycle repeats until the design actually solves the problem.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Why do you think engineers build a prototype first instead of just building the real thing right away?"
🌉
Section 2
Three Types of Crossings

Engineers designed three main kinds of wildlife crossingsWildlife CrossingA structure — like a bridge or tunnel — built to help animals safely cross roads.. Each one works best for different animals.

Overpasses are wide, grassy bridges built over roads. Large animals like elk and bears walk across the top. Underpasses are tunnels below the road — foxes and smaller animals use these. Culverts are small pipes through the road — perfect for frogs, otters, and salamanders. Tap the diagram below to explore each one.

⚙️
Section 3
What Makes a Crossing Work?

A crossing only works if animals actually use it. Engineers study animal behavior — the widths animals need, the sounds they avoid, and the paths they naturally take. Then they match the design to those needs.

That's why engineering isn't just building — it's testing, watching, and improving. Real-World Connection: In Banff, Canada, engineers installed cameras at their wildlife crossings. It took time, but eventually bears, elk, and wolves all started using them regularly.

⭐ Key Concept

The best engineering solutions don't come from a single good idea — they come from testing, finding what breaks, and revising. That process of building, testing, and improving is called iteration, and it's how every real wildlife crossing gets made.

A prototype that fails teaches an engineer more than a design that was never tested at all.

🤯
Amazing Engineering Fact
Engineers in the Netherlands built a wildlife tunnel called the Natuurbrug Zanderij Crailoo — it's 800 meters long and crosses over a highway, a railroad, AND a canal all at once. It took over a decade to plan and build. Badgers, deer, and foxes now use it every day. It didn't work perfectly at first — engineers kept improving it until animals trusted it.
Curious? Go Deeper

How do engineers know when a crossing design is actually working? They don't just watch — they use motion-sensor cameras installed at the crossing entrances. The cameras record every animal that approaches or walks through. Scientists then sort through thousands of photos and videos to count which species used the crossing, how often, and at what times of day.

That data drives the next iteration. If a crossing was designed for deer but the cameras show foxes using it instead, engineers might adjust the height or width of a future design. If animals approach but then turn back, it might mean the crossing feels too exposed — so engineers add more vegetation to make it feel safer. Every iteration is informed by evidence, not guesses.

🛠 Engineering Challenge: Design a Wildlife Crossing
Design, build, test, and evaluate your own crossing structure.
⏱ 25 min
✅

Your criteria: The crossing must connect both sides. An animal (small object) must be able to pass through or over. It must stay standing on its own.

🚧

Your constraints: Use only cardboard, craft sticks, and tape. No wider than 30 cm. Must be built in 20 minutes or less.

Work through each step in order. Write in your science notebook as you go.

1

Understand the Problem: Read the criteria and constraints above. In your notebook, write: Which animal will your crossing help? What type of crossing (overpass, underpass, culvert) will you design and why?

2

Brainstorm & Sketch: Draw at least 2 different crossing designs in your notebook. Label the parts of each design. Then circle the design you want to build.

3

Build Your Prototype: Use your materials to build the crossing you sketched. Work carefully — measure before you cut, and test pieces before taping them permanently.

4

Test Your Design: Use a small object (a pebble or eraser) as your "animal." Can it travel from one side to the other? Does the crossing hold? Does it stay within 30 cm? Record what you see.

5

Evaluate & Improve: Check your design against each criterion. What worked? What didn't? If you had more time and materials, what would you change first?

Think Like an Engineer
"Real engineers never finish on the first try. What is the one thing you would change about your crossing, and why would that change make it better?"

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See what you remember before the assignment.
1. What is the main purpose of a wildlife crossing?
ATo give cars a faster path through the forest
BTo help animals safely travel across a road without getting hurt
CTo block animals from entering roads by building a fence
🌿 Yes! Wildlife crossings are built so animals can safely get from one side of a road to the other — solving the problem that roads create for wildlife.
Think again. A wildlife crossing is built for animals, not cars. What problem does a road create for animals trying to reach food, water, or shelter?
2. An engineer is designing an underpass tunnel for foxes. What should she test FIRST?
AWhether the tunnel looks nice from the outside
BWhether it is cheaper than an overpass
CWhether a fox-sized animal can safely pass through it
🛠 Exactly! Engineers always test against the criteria first. The crossing has to work for the animal — that's the whole point.
Think about the criteria for this crossing. What is the most important thing it must actually do? That's what gets tested first.
3. Which of these is an example of a constraint in engineering?
AYou may only use cardboard and tape to build the crossing
BThe crossing must allow an animal to pass from one side to the other
CThe crossing must stay standing on its own
⭐ Right! A constraint limits what you can use or do. "Only cardboard and tape" is a limit on your materials — that's a constraint. Options B and C are criteria — things the design must achieve.
Options B and C describe things the design must do — those are criteria. A constraint is a limit on your resources or rules. Which option limits what you're allowed to use?
