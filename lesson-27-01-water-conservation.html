📖 The Lesson: Water Conservation & Engineering
Read carefully — every detail matters for the design challenge ahead.
⏱ 12 min
💧
Section 1
Earth Has Less Freshwater Than You Think

Most of Earth is covered in water — but almost all of it is saltwater in the oceans. Only about 3% of all Earth's water is freshwaterFreshwaterWater with little or no salt — found in rivers, lakes, and underground.. And most of that is locked in glaciers and ice caps.

What's actually available for people to drink, grow food with, and use every day? Less than 1% of all the water on Earth. That tiny amount has to supply every person, every farm, and every city on the planet.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If less than 1% of all Earth's water is available to use, what do you think happens when people use more than nature can replace?"
☀️
Section 2
How Freshwater Gets Used Up

Two big things shrink freshwater supplies: droughts and overuse. A droughtDroughtA long stretch of time with very little rain — rivers drop and wells run low. can last months or even years, leaving rivers and lakes dangerously low.

Overuse is just as serious. When people pump water out of an aquiferAquiferAn underground layer of rock or soil that stores freshwater — pumped up through wells. faster than rain can refill it, that underground supply slowly shrinks. Here in Florida, millions of people depend on the Floridan Aquifer. When too much gets pumped out during dry summers, it takes a very long time to recover.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If a drought AND overuse are both happening at the same time, what do you predict would happen to the aquifer?"
🛠️
Section 3
What Engineers Do About It

Engineers don't just hope water problems solve themselves — they design solutions. A good engineering solution has criteriaCriteriaThe specific things a solution must do to count as successful.: specific things it must do to count as successful. It also has constraints: limits like cost, materials, or space.

Some solutions collect rain before it becomes runoffRunoffWater that flows off land after rain, picking up whatever it passes over. and flows away. Others deliver water exactly where plants need it so none is wasted. Bigger projects — like restoring the Everglades wetlands — work at a landscape scale, letting nature filter and store water for entire regions.

Real-World Connection: The Everglades was once heavily drained for farming. Engineers are now rebuilding it to restore Florida's natural freshwater storage — one of the largest ecological engineering projects in U.S. history.

💡 Key Concept

Freshwater is a limited resource. Engineers identify criteria and constraints to design solutions that help communities use freshwater wisely — especially when droughts or overuse put supplies at risk.

🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
Only about 0.3% of all Earth's water sits in rivers and lakes — the places we most easily reach. Every person on the planet shares that tiny sliver. If it disappeared tomorrow, there would be nothing left to drink or grow food with.
Curious? Go Deeper

The Floridan Aquifer stretches under most of Florida and parts of Georgia, Alabama, and South Carolina. It's one of the most productive aquifers in the world — but it doesn't refill quickly. Rain soaks in slowly through sand and limestone, a process that can take decades for deep water to be replaced.

For most of Florida's history, the Everglades acted as a giant sponge. Rainwater flooded across millions of acres of wetland, slowly filtering down to refill the aquifer. Then, in the 1900s, engineers drained much of the Everglades for farms and cities. The aquifer began to shrink. Now, a massive project called the Comprehensive Everglades Restoration Plan (CERP) is working to undo that damage — re-flooding wetlands and rebuilding the natural system that recharges Florida's water supply.

That's engineering thinking at its biggest: not just fixing a small problem, but redesigning an entire landscape so it can sustain people and nature for generations.

🔬 Design Evaluation: Judge a Water-Saving Solution
Use criteria and constraints to decide which solution works better.
⏱ 15 min
📋

The Scenario: A neighborhood in central Florida gets its water from the Floridan Aquifer. Every summer, droughts dry things up and the aquifer drops. The community wants to reduce how much aquifer water they use. Two engineering solutions have been proposed. Your job: evaluate both using criteria and constraints.

🖼️
Suggested Image
A clear side-by-side illustration: left side shows a rain barrel collecting roof runoff from a downspout (Solution A); right side shows drip irrigation lines running along a garden row (Solution B). Simple, labeled diagram style.
images/27-01-rain-barrel-solution.jpg

💧 Solution A — Rain Barrel

Barrels placed under roof downspouts collect rainwater. That stored water is used for gardens and outdoor tasks — so less aquifer water gets pumped. Cost: about $75 per barrel. Works best during and right after rain events.

🌱 Solution B — Drip Irrigation

Thin hoses deliver water directly to plant roots, one drop at a time — using up to 50% less water than a sprinkler. The system still uses aquifer water, but much less of it. Cost: about $150 per garden. Works any time plants need water.

✅

Criteria (what the solution MUST do):
1. Reduce how much water is pumped from the aquifer
2. Work during or after a drought when rain is scarce
3. Stay within a neighborhood budget of $200 or less per household

1. What is an aquifer?
AA river that flows underground and comes up as a spring
BAn underground layer of rock or soil that stores freshwater
CA tank that engineers build to hold drinking water
💧 Exactly right! An aquifer is a natural underground storage layer. Rain slowly soaks down and fills it up — but it can take a long time to refill once it runs low.
Not quite. An aquifer is natural — it's not a river or a human-built tank. It's an underground layer of rock or soil where freshwater gets stored. Rain seeps down into it slowly over time.
2. A drought lasts for several months with almost no rain. What happens to the aquifer if people keep pumping the same amount of water from it?
AThe aquifer fills up faster because rain is being saved
BNothing changes — aquifers don't depend on rain
CThe aquifer slowly shrinks because it's being used faster than it's being refilled
🌿 Exactly! Without rain to refill it, and with people still pumping water out, the aquifer loses more than it gains. That's why conservation matters most during a drought.
Think about it this way: if you keep spending money but stop earning any, what happens to your savings? The aquifer works the same way — no rain coming in, but water still going out means it shrinks.
3. A town wants to reduce how much water they pump from the aquifer during summer. Which solution better meets that criterion?
ADigging a deeper well to reach more aquifer water
BInstalling rain barrels to collect roof runoff for outdoor use
CBuilding a bigger water tower to store more aquifer water
💡 Yes! Rain barrels collect water that would otherwise run off. Using that stored water for gardens and outdoor tasks means less aquifer water gets pumped — that directly meets the criterion.
Think about what the criterion actually asks for: reduce aquifer pumping. Digging a deeper well or building a bigger tower still uses aquifer water. Rain barrels use a different source — rooftop rainwater — so they actually reduce how much gets pumped.
