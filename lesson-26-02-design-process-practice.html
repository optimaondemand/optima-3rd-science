📖 The Lesson: How Engineers Solve Problems
Read carefully — this is how real engineers think.
⏱ 10 min
🔎
Section 1
More Than One Idea

Engineers don't just build the first thing that comes to mind and call it done. They come up with multiple solutionsSolutionA design that solves a problem. — two or three or even more — and then decide which one is best.

Every solution is judged against criteriaCriteriaThe features a design must have to succeed. and constraintsConstraintLimits on what you can use, like materials or time.. Criteria are the must-haves. Constraints are the limits.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If your criteria is 'holds at least 10 pennies' and your constraint is 'one sheet of paper,' what counts as a winning design?"
🔨
Section 2
Build It, Then Test It

A prototypePrototypeA first-try model built to test an idea. is a test model. It's not meant to be perfect — it's meant to teach you something. When a bridge prototype sags under three pennies, that's useful information. It shows where the design needs work.

A good test changes only one thing at a time. That way you know exactly what caused the result. If you change the fold and the number of tape pieces at the same time, you won't know which change made the difference.

📊
Section 3
Compare and Improve

After testing, you compare your results. Which design held more? Which used fewer materials? Which came closest to meeting the criteria? The better solution becomes your starting point for the next round of improvements.

Real-World Connection: Car designers build dozens of prototype models before a single car reaches the road. Each one teaches them what works and what doesn't.

🗺 Tap a step to learn about it
Tap any step to see what happens there.
The engineering design process is a cycle — engineers keep going around until the solution meets the criteria.
⭐ Key Concept

Engineers always compare solutions by testing them against the same criteria and constraints. The test results tell you which design is closer to solving the problem — and what to try next.

🤯
Amazing Engineering Fact
Engineers at NASA tested the first moon rocket more than 2,000 times before launch. Each test found something small to fix — and those small fixes kept the astronauts safe.
Curious? Go Deeper

Why does folding paper make it stronger? When you fold a flat sheet into an accordion, you're turning it into a structure with many small triangles and ridges. Those shapes distribute weight more evenly than a flat surface can.

This is the same reason corrugated cardboard — the wavy paper inside a cardboard box — is so much stronger than two flat layers alone. Engineers use this idea in bridges, airplanes, and buildings.

The word for this is structural design: using shape, not just materials, to make something stronger. A flat piece of paper and a folded piece of paper are made of the same stuff — but the shape makes all the difference.

🔧 Engineering Challenge: Paper Bridge Test
Build two solutions, test both, compare results, improve.
⏱ 20 min
🎯

The Problem: Design a paper bridge that spans a gap between two books and holds as many pennies as possible — using only one sheet of paper and up to 4 small pieces of tape.

📋 Criteria (must-haves)

The bridge must span the gap without touching the table. It must hold as many pennies as possible before sagging too much to count.

⛔ Constraints (limits)

One sheet of paper per design. Up to 4 pieces of tape per design. The gap between books must stay the same for both tests.

⚙️ Set Up

Place two books flat on a table, about one hand-width apart (roughly 6 inches). Keep them in this same spot for all your tests — that's how you make it a fair test.

Follow each step. Record what you observe as you go — don't wait until the end.

1

Build Design 1 — Flat Bridge: Lay the first sheet of paper flat across the gap between your two books. Use up to 2 pieces of tape to hold each end down. Don't fold or shape it at all.

2

Test Design 1: Place pennies in the center, one at a time. Count out loud as you go. Stop when the bridge sags so much it touches the table. Record how many pennies it held in your chart below.

3

Build Design 2 — Folded Bridge: Take your second sheet of paper. Fold it back and forth like an accordion — at least 4 folds. Lay it across the gap with the folds running across the span. Tape each end down.

4

Test Design 2: Repeat the same penny test. Record your results in the same chart. Keep the setup exactly the same — same books, same gap, same size pennies.

5

Improve: Look at your results. Take your stronger design and try one change — more folds, fewer folds, a different number of tape pieces, or a different fold direction. Build it and test it a third time. Record the result.

🌲
Think Like an Engineer
"Why is it important to keep the gap the same for all three tests? What would happen to your results if you changed it?"
