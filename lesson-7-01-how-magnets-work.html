📖 The Lesson: How Magnets Work
Read carefully — and stop to think at each question.
⏱ 12 min
💫
Section 1
A Force You Can't See

Hold a magnet near a paper clip. Even before they touch, you can feel something pulling. That invisible pull is called magnetismmagnetismThe invisible force a magnet creates around it — it can push or pull without touching..

Magnets are different from most forces. They work at a distance — through air, through water, even through a thin piece of cardboard. The force doesn't need contact to work.

🤔
Stop and Think
"Can you think of another force that works without touching? Or is magnetism the only one you know?"
🔴🔵
Section 2
Poles: Attract and Repel

Every magnet has two ends called polespolesThe two ends of a magnet — north and south — where the force is strongest.: a north pole and a south pole. The force is strongest at these ends.

Here's the rule that matters: opposite poles attract, and matching poles repel. North and south pull toward each other. North and north push away. South and south push away too.

💡

Try to picture it: two magnets facing the same way will push each other apart so hard you can barely bring them together. Flip one around and they snap toward each other. Same magnets — just a different direction.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If you had three magnets in a row, what would happen if they all had the same poles facing the same direction?"
🔩
Section 3
What Does a Magnet Attract?

Not everything sticks to a magnet. Only magnetic materialsmagnetic materialA material, like iron or steel, that is attracted to a magnet. are pulled toward it. Iron and steel are the most common ones. That's why a fridge door holds magnets — it's made of steel.

Plastic, wood, rubber, aluminum, and glass are all nonmagnetic. A magnet held near a plastic cup does nothing at all.

Real-World Connection: Scrap metal recyclers use powerful magnets on cranes to sort steel from other materials. It's faster than sorting by hand — and magnets never get tired.

⭐ Key Concept

Magnets exert a force without touching. That force is strongest at the poles. Opposite poles pull toward each other; matching poles push apart.

Only magnetic materials — like iron and steel — respond to this force. The cause (pole orientation and distance) determines the effect (direction and strength of the push or pull).

🤯
Amazing Science Fact
The Earth itself is a giant magnet. Its iron core acts like an enormous bar magnet, which is why a compass needle always points north. Every compass on Earth is responding to magnetic forces coming from deep underground.
Curious? Go Deeper

What is a magnetic field? The invisible region around a magnet where its force can be felt is called a magnetic field. If you put iron filings on a piece of paper and hold a magnet underneath, the filings arrange themselves into curved lines — those are the field lines. Scientists use diagrams of these lines to understand and predict where the force will be strong or weak.

Distance matters a lot. The magnetic force gets much weaker as you move farther from the magnet. Double the distance and the force drops off sharply — that's why a magnet can pick up a paper clip from 2 centimeters away, but not from 20.

Some materials, like iron, can actually become temporary magnets themselves when a strong magnet is nearby. This is called induced magnetism. Take the strong magnet away and the iron goes back to being ordinary. That's different from a permanent magnet, which holds its magnetism on its own.

🔬 Investigation: What Does a Magnet Attract?
Test objects. Record what you find.
⏱ 18 min
🎒

You'll need: one magnet · 8–10 small objects to test (paper clips, rubber bands, a coin, a pencil, a nail, a piece of foil, a plastic cap, a wooden craft stick) · your science notebook · a pencil

Before you start testing, make a prediction. Do scientists guess before they test? Yes — that's called a hypothesis. Then the test tells you if you were right.

1

Set up your data table. Draw a table in your notebook with three columns: Object, My Prediction, and What Happened. List your 8–10 objects in the first column.

2

Make your predictions first — before you test. For each object, write "magnetic" or "nonmagnetic" in the prediction column. Think about what the object is made of.

3

Test each object. Hold the magnet about 2 cm away. Then move it slowly closer. Record in the third column whether the object was attracted, not attracted, or weakly attracted.

4

Test the poles. Take two magnets. Bring the same ends (both north or both south) toward each other. Then flip one and try again. Write what you observe — push or pull?

5

Draw and label two sketches in your notebook: one showing attraction, one showing repulsion. Label which poles are facing each other in each drawing.

📝 Record Your Thinking

In your science notebook, answer these questions:

  • Which objects were magnetic? What do they have in common?
  • Were any of your predictions wrong? What surprised you?
  • Tell the story of what happened when you tested the two magnets together — from beginning to end, in your own words.
💭
Think It Through
"What pattern did you notice about the materials that were magnetic? Could you use that pattern to predict whether a new object is magnetic — before you test it?"
💭
Think It Through
"What would happen to the repulsion if you moved the two same poles closer together? What do you think would happen to the force?"

1. Two magnets are pushed toward each other and they push apart. What are the poles doing?
A Opposite poles are facing each other.
B Matching poles are facing each other.
C The magnets have lost their magnetism.
✅ Right! When matching poles face each other — north-north or south-south — the magnets repel. That pushing force is called repulsion.
Not quite. Think about what "repel" means. Matching poles (like north facing north) push each other away.
2. You hold a magnet near a wooden pencil. What happens?
A The pencil is strongly attracted.
B The pencil is weakly attracted.
C Nothing happens — wood is nonmagnetic.
✅ Exactly. Wood is not a magnetic material, so the magnet has no effect on it at all. Only iron and steel are attracted to magnets.
Check the lesson. Is wood a magnetic material? Think about what materials actually respond to magnets.
3. Where is the magnetic force strongest on a bar magnet?
A At the two poles (ends) of the magnet.
B In the middle of the magnet.
C Equally strong everywhere on the magnet.
✅ Yes — the poles are where the force is strongest. That's why you pick things up with the end of a magnet, not the middle.
Review Section 2 in the lesson. The force is not equal everywhere — think about where a magnet picks up the most iron filings.
