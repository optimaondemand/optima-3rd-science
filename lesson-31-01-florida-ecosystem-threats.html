📖 The Lesson: Florida's Ecosystems Under Threat
Read and think carefully — science is all about noticing!
⏱ 12 min
🌿
Section 1
Florida's Wild Places Are in Trouble

Florida is home to some of the most unique ecosystemsEcosystemAll the living things in a place and the non-living things they depend on — water, soil, sunlight. in North America. Wetlands like the Everglades, mangrove forests along the coast, and scrub forests inland support hundreds of species.

But scientists are watching these places change. Water is getting murkier. Animals are losing their homes. Some species that have lived here for thousands of years are disappearing.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If a Florida wetland lost all its native plants, what do you think would happen to the animals that depend on them?"
⚠️
Section 2
Three Big Threats

Pollution happens when harmful materials enter water or soil. Farm fertilizers and lawn chemicals wash into lakes and rivers during storms. This runoffRunoffWater that flows across land after rain, carrying chemicals into rivers, lakes, and wetlands. causes algae to grow out of control — blocking sunlight and removing oxygen from the water.

Invasive species are plants and animals that don't naturally belong in Florida. The Burmese python was brought as a pet. Brazilian pepper trees were planted for landscaping. Both now spread and push out native species.

📷 Invasive Species · Florida Everglades
📷
Suggested Photo Needed
Photorealistic nature photo prompt: A large Burmese python coiled in Florida Everglades sawgrass and wetland vegetation — partially camouflaged, clearly visible, natural lighting. OR: A dense thicket of Brazilian pepper trees with red berries overtaking native Florida shrubs. Wild, real, no people, no overlays.
images/31-01-invasive-species-florida.jpg
An invasive species in the Everglades. This animal doesn't belong here — and it's causing serious harm to native wildlife that evolved alongside Florida's natural ecosystem.

Habitat loss occurs when natural land is cleared for buildings, farms, or roads. When a wetland is drained or a forest is bulldozed, the animals that lived there have nowhere to go.

🌱
Section 3
People Are Working on Solutions

People caused many of these problems — and people are working to fix them. Engineers design filtration systems that clean runoffRunoffWater that flows across land after rain, carrying chemicals into rivers, lakes, and wetlands. before it reaches wetlands. ConservationConservationThe work of protecting and restoring nature so ecosystems stay healthy. groups remove invasive plants and animals and replant native ones.

Laws protect wild areas from development. Florida has more protected land than almost any other state — and scientists and volunteers work every day to slow these threats.

Real-World Connection: In the Everglades, engineers are rebuilding water flow systems to reduce pollution and restore the natural balance that native wildlife needs.

Florida ecosystem zones — from healthy wetland to habitat lost, and back to protected land. Each zone shows a different condition and its effect on wildlife.
⭐ Key Concept

Florida's ecosystems face three major threats: pollution, invasive species, and habitat loss. Each one disrupts the balance that native plants and animals depend on.

People caused these problems — and people are designing solutions to slow the damage and help ecosystems recover.

🤯
Amazing Florida Fact
Scientists tracking wildlife in the Everglades found that raccoon, opossum, and rabbit populations dropped by over 90% in areas with Burmese pythons. One invasive species changed the whole ecosystem — just by eating.
Curious? Go Deeper

The Everglades once covered about 4,500 square miles of southern Florida. In the 1900s, farmers and developers drained and rerouted its water to make room for agriculture and cities. Wading bird populations dropped by over 90% within decades.

Today, engineers are working on one of the largest environmental restoration projects ever attempted. They're rebuilding water flow patterns, removing old canals, and constructing filtration marshes to clean polluted water before it enters the wetland.

Results come slowly — ecosystems don't heal overnight. But scientists are seeing water quality improve in some areas. It's proof that even large-scale damage can be addressed when science and engineering work together.
