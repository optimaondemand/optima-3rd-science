📖 Background: What Do Engineers Do?
Read before you build.
⏱ 8 min
🔧
Section 1
Engineers Solve Real Problems

Engineers are problem-solvers. When storms damage buildings, engineers ask: what would make this better?

In Florida, hurricaneshurricaneA powerful storm with very strong rotating winds and heavy rain. can push walls, tear off roofs, and flood homes. Engineers study what breaks — and design buildings that hold up better the next time.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If you wanted to build something that stays standing in strong wind, where would you start?"
🏠
Section 2
Features That Help Structures Survive

Not all buildings are built the same. Some features make a structurestructureSomething built to hold a shape or serve a purpose. more likely to survive a storm.

Things engineers often consider: a wide, stable base to resist tipping; a sloped roof so wind slides over instead of pushing in; a raised foundation to stay above floodwater; and cross-bracing inside walls to keep them from collapsing sideways.

🔁
Section 3
The Design Process

Engineers don't just build and hope. They follow a process: define the problem → plan → build → test → redesign.

Testing is the most important step. It shows where a design breaks down. That information is not a failure — it's exactly what engineers need to make the next version better.

Real-World Connection: After Hurricane Andrew hit Florida in 1992, engineers studied what broke. Florida changed its building rules. Newer buildings survived the next big storms much better than the old ones did.

⭐ Key Concept

Engineers can't stop a hurricane, but they can design structures that reduce the damage. A good design starts with understanding what the storm will do — and building in features that resist those forces.

🤯
Engineering Fact
After Hurricane Andrew destroyed over 125,000 homes in Florida in 1992, engineers redesigned the building rules. Homes built under the new code survived the next major storms far better — roof damage dropped by more than half.
Curious? Go Deeper

Hurricane Andrew and the Engineering Reset. When Andrew hit South Florida, it was one of the most destructive storms in U.S. history. Thousands of houses lost their roofs — not because the storm was unusual, but because the buildings weren't built to code. Engineers and inspectors discovered that many roofs had been nailed incorrectly, with too few fasteners.

Florida rewrote its building code in 1994. The new rules required stronger roof-to-wall connections, impact-resistant windows, and cross-bracing in wall frames. When Hurricane Charley hit in 2004, homes built under the new code fared significantly better than the older ones — even though Charley's winds were very strong. The data showed engineering decisions made years earlier actually saved buildings.

This is why engineers test designs and review failures. Real improvement comes from looking honestly at what broke — and designing to prevent it next time.

🏗️ Engineering Challenge: Build a Safer Structure
Define · Plan · Build · Test · Redesign
⏱ 30 min
🎒

Materials available: paper, cardboard, straws, craft sticks, tape (masking or scotch), scissors. Use only what's at your station. That's your constraint — make the most of it.

Work through each step in order. Write and sketch in your science notebook as you go — your notes are part of your final submission.

1

Define the Problem. Write this in your notebook: What is the challenge? What does your structure need to survive?

List your criteria (what must it do?) and your constraints (what limits do you have?). Be specific. "It should not fall over when I blow on it" is better than "it should be strong."

2

Plan Your Design. Sketch your structure in your notebook. Label at least 4 features and explain next to each one how it will help.

Think about what you learned: sloped roofs, raised foundations, wide bases, cross-bracing. Which ones make sense for your design? You don't have to use all of them — choose what fits.

3

Build Your Prototype. Use only the materials available at your station. Build according to your sketch.

If something doesn't work while you're building, note it down. Changes made during building are normal — real engineers adjust as they go. Just write down what you changed and why.

4

Test Your Structure. Simulate two types of storm forces:

🌬️ Wind: Blow on your structure steadily, or use a fan. Does it lean, shift, or fall?

💧 Water: Spray or pour a small amount of water at the base. Does water reach the "floor" of your structure?

5

Record What Happened. In your notebook, describe both tests specifically.

What held up? What gave way first? Where did your structure show its weakest point? Write exactly what you saw — not just "it worked" or "it broke," but where and how.

6

Redesign. Identify the weakest part of your structure. Write one specific change you would make and explain why it should help.

If you have time, rebuild and retest that part. Compare your two results. Did the change make a difference?

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Three questions — pick the best answer for each.

1. What do criteria describe in an engineering design problem?

A
The materials you are allowed to use.
B
What the solution needs to do to solve the problem.
C
How long the project will take.
✅ Exactly! Criteria describe the goals — what success looks like for your design.
Not quite. Criteria tell you what the design needs to do — the goals for a successful solution.

2. Why is testing a prototype so important in engineering?

A
It shows that the design is finished and ready to use.
B
It reveals weaknesses so the engineer can improve the design.
C
It decides which color to paint the structure.
✅ Right! Finding weaknesses is the whole point. That's what makes the next version better.
Try again. Testing isn't the end — it's how you find out what needs to be fixed.

3. Which building feature would most help a structure survive flooding?

A
Extra-large windows on every wall.
B
A raised foundation that lifts the floor above the ground.
C
A very flat roof to hold rainwater.
✅ Good thinking! Raising the floor means floodwater can pass underneath without getting inside.
Think about where floodwater goes. A raised foundation lifts the floor above the water level.
