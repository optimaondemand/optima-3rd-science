📖 The Lesson: Engineering for Wildlife
Read and think carefully — good engineers are careful readers too.
⏱ 12 min
🔍
Section 1
What Is a Design Problem?

Engineers start by noticing a problem — something in the world that isn't working the way it should. A design problemDesign ProblemA real need or challenge that can be solved by creating or improving something. is a real need that can be solved by building or changing something.

Before touching any materials, an engineer has to understand the problem clearly. What is going wrong? Who or what is being hurt by it? Animals face many problems caused by human-made things — busy roads, fences, bright lights, dams — and engineers can help fix them.

🤔
Stop and Think
"A road is built through the middle of a forest. What problems could that create for the animals living there?"
⚖️
Section 2
Criteria and Constraints

Once an engineer understands the problem, they figure out two important things: criteriaCriteriaWhat the solution must do to work — the goals. and constraintsConstraintA limit on a solution, like cost, time, or available materials..

Criteria are the goals — what the solution must do. A wildlife bridge must be wide enough for a bear to walk across comfortably. Constraints are the limits — the things you have to work around. Maybe the bridge can only cost a certain amount, or must be built in six months before the rainy season.

Good engineers think about both before they design anything. A solution that meets the criteria but ignores the constraints usually won't get built.

🌎
Section 3
Real Solutions for Real Animals

Engineers have already designed many solutions to help animals. In some places, tunnels run under busy roads so box turtles and salamanders can cross without getting hurt. Manatees in Florida are protected by slow-speed zones and specially designed boat bumpers. Near beaches where sea turtles hatch at night, special lights are angled away from the water so hatchlings aren't confused.

Each solution started the same way: an engineer noticed a problem, figured out what the solution needed to do, and worked within real limits to build it.

Real-World Connection: Wildlife crossing structures exist across North America — from small amphibian tunnels under two-lane roads to massive grass-covered bridges over major highways.

🗺 Two engineering solutions — one road
Engineers designed two solutions for the same road: a wide overpass bridge for large animals and a small tunnel underneath for turtles and other small animals.
⭐ Key Concept

A design problem has two parts: criteria (what the solution must do) and constraints (the limits it must work within). Understanding both is what separates a solution that works from one that doesn't.

Engineers who protect wildlife use this same process — the science just also happens to save lives.

🤯
Mind-Blowing Fact
In Banff, Canada, grassy bridges cross a major highway. Over 20 years, more than 100,000 animals — bears, wolves, cougars, and elk — have used them. Wildlife-vehicle collisions dropped by over 80%.
Curious? Go Deeper

Wildlife corridors are strips of natural land connecting two larger habitats — like hallways between rooms. Without them, animals get cut off from food, mates, and shelter. Small, isolated populations can slowly disappear even without being hunted or harmed directly.

Engineers who design corridors have to think carefully about which animals will use them. A tunnel small enough for a box turtle won't work for a Florida black bear. Some corridors are built for one species; others are wide enough for dozens. That's a genuinely tricky engineering challenge — one design doesn't fit all.

In Florida, wildlife underpasses under highways have been built specifically for the endangered Florida panther. Scientists track which animals use them using motion-sensor cameras. That data helps engineers improve the next one.

🔬 Design Problem Sort
Read each problem. Find the criteria and constraints.
⏱ 15 min

Engineers always start by understanding the problem. Read each wildlife problem below. For each one, write in your science notebook:

📓

For each problem, write:
• What is the problem?
• One criteria (what would a good solution need to do?)
• One constraint (what might limit what engineers can build?)

1

🐢 The Turtle Road: A two-lane road runs along the edge of a pond. Every spring, box turtles cross the road to reach their nesting area on the other side. Dozens are hit by cars each year. The turtles have used this route for hundreds of years.

2

🐦 The Window Problem: A school building has large glass windows that face a bird migration path. Every fall, birds fly into the glass because they can't see it. Up to 1 billion birds die from window collisions in the U.S. every year. The school can't replace all the windows — they're too expensive.

3

🐢 The Turtle Beach Lights: A beach resort installed bright stadium-style lights facing the ocean. When sea turtle eggs hatch at night, the hatchlings are supposed to crawl toward the brightest light — which is normally the moon on the water. The resort lights are confusing them, and they crawl the wrong way. The resort can't turn off all the lights for safety reasons.

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you remember.
1. What is a "criteria" in engineering design?
AA limit on what materials you can use
BWhat the solution must do to solve the problem
CThe animal that needs protection
🛠️ Yes! Criteria are the goals — what the solution needs to accomplish. Constraints are the limits.
Think back to Section 2. Criteria and constraints are a pair — one is the goal, one is the limit. Which is which?
2. A wildlife bridge can only be built in 4 months before the rainy season begins. What kind of engineering limit is this?
AA criteria
BA constraint
CA design problem
🌿 Right! Time limits are a classic constraint — something the engineer has to work within, not a goal to meet.
A constraint is a limit — time, money, materials. Is a 4-month window a goal or a limit?
3. Bright stadium lights are confusing baby sea turtles that hatch at night. Which solution shows good engineering thinking?
ADesign turtle-safe lights that aim away from the water, so turtles aren't confused
BMove the turtles to a different beach far from the resort
CTurn all the lights off every night at the whole resort
⭐ Exactly! A good engineering solution solves the problem and works within the constraints — the resort can't turn off all its lights, but it can redesign them.
Think about the constraints — the resort can't turn off all its lights for safety, and moving the turtles doesn't fix the root problem. What solution works within those limits?
