Amazing Science Fact
Jane Goodall was about your age when she started watching animals in her backyard. She sat quietly, noticed things nobody else did, and kept a notebook. That notebook habit turned into 60 years of discoveries about chimpanzees.
Curious? Go Deeper

Real scientists don't work alone. They share their findings, ask each other questions, and build on each other's work. Every investigation you did this year followed the same basic steps that professional researchers use.

The biggest difference between a 3rd grader and a professional scientist isn't the equipment — it's practice. Scientists have been asking questions and collecting evidence for years. And you've been doing it all year. That's a real start.

Pick Your Scenario

Scenario A — Mountain Park
A wildfire swept through part of a mountain forest last summer. Now spring is here. What will happen to the animals and plants that lived there?

Scenario B — Coastal Park
A new parking lot is being built near a beach where sea turtles nest every year. How might this change affect the turtles and other animals nearby?

Winters in a forest park are getting shorter and warmer. The snow melts earlier every year. What might happen to the plants and animals that depend on cold winters?

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how you think like a scientist.
1. A scientist notices that plants near a river grow taller than plants far from it. What should she do next?
ADecide the river causes tall plants and stop investigating.
BIgnore the observation because it might be a coincidence.
CCollect more data — measure the plants and the distance from the river to look for a pattern.
🌈 Yes! One observation isn't enough. Real scientists collect more data and look for patterns before drawing conclusions.
Scientists don't stop at one observation or ignore what they see. They collect more data to find out if the pattern is real.
2. Which of these is the best example of using evidence to support an idea?
A"I think magnets are cool because they stick to the fridge."
B"The magnet attracted the paperclip from 2 cm away but not from 10 cm. Distance affects the force."
C"Magnets probably work because of electricity inside them."
⭐ That's evidence-based thinking — a specific observation that supports a clear idea.
Evidence means specific data or observations, not opinions or guesses. Look for the answer with a real measurement.
3. You studied weather, ecosystems, forces, and engineering this year. What do all of these have in common?
AScientists study all of them by asking questions, collecting evidence, and looking for patterns.
BThey are all about animals and plants.
CThey all require the same kind of equipment to study.
🌟 Exactly! The process is what connects all science — asking, observing, collecting evidence, and explaining.
Think bigger than the topic. What process did you use in every single investigation this year?
