Section 1
How Magnetic Force Works

A magnet creates a magnetic forcemagnetic forceA push or pull between a magnet and a magnetic material — no touching required. around it. That force can pull certain metal objects toward the magnet without any contact.

Not all metals are magnetic. Paper clips and washers work. Aluminum foil does not. Knowing which materials respond to a magnet is part of solving the problem.

Section 2
The Engineering Design Process

Engineers don't just build — they follow a process. They start with a problem, plan a solution, build and test it, then redesignredesignTo change a design after testing, using evidence to make it better. based on what the data shows.

Today you'll follow that same cycle: Ask → Plan → Build → Test → Improve. Each round of testing gives you evidence for what to change next.

🔍
Section 3
Why Testing Matters

A prototypeprototypeA first working model — built to test and learn from, not to be perfect. is never meant to be perfect. It's meant to teach you something. When you test it and record data, you find exactly where it fails — and that tells you what to fix.

Real-World Connection: Engineers who build cranes for recycling yards use magnets to sort metal from trash. They test different magnet strengths and arm lengths to lift the most material safely.

The Engineering Design Cycle — five steps that repeat until your solution works well.
⭐ Key Concept

Magnetic force acts at a distance. A magnet can pull on certain metal objects without touching them. Engineers use this property to design tools that pick up, sort, or hold metal objects.

Testing a prototype and collecting data is how engineers decide what to improve. One small change at a time gives you clear evidence of what's working.

🤯
Amazing Engineering Fact
Scrapyard cranes use electromagnets powerful enough to lift entire cars. When the power switches off, the metal just drops. Engineers use the same magnetic-force idea you're exploring today — just at a much bigger scale.
Curious? Go Deeper

What makes some magnets stronger than others? It comes down to the material. Neodymium magnets (made from a rare metal) are the strongest permanent magnets you can hold in your hand — a small one can lift 10 times its own weight. The disc-shaped magnet in a craft-store pack is much weaker.

Distance also changes magnetic force dramatically. Move a magnet twice as far away from an object and the force doesn't just halve — it drops much more sharply. That's why shortening the string on your rescue tool makes such a noticeable difference in how many clips it can grab.

🧲 Engineering Challenge: Magnetic Rescue Tool
Design, build, test, and improve a magnetic tool.
⏱ 25 min
🎒

Materials available: 1–2 magnets · 2 craft sticks · string · tape · small cardboard pieces · paper clips and metal washers for testing

Work through each step below. Follow the engineering design cycle — and remember, your first version doesn't have to be perfect. That's what testing is for.

1

Define the Problem: Write in your notebook — what does your tool need to do? What counts as success? Try writing one sentence that starts: "My tool should be able to…"

2

Plan Your Design: Draw your tool and label the parts. Mark where the magnet goes, how the string is attached, and how you'll hold it. Use the available materials — don't build yet.

3

Build Your Prototype: Assemble your tool as close to your plan as you can. Tape securely — the magnet needs to hang freely at the end, not be pressed against the stick.

4

Test Your Tool: Lower the magnet over a pile of paper clips and washers. Count exactly how many of each you picked up. Write the numbers in your data table under "Trial 1." Be specific — "3 paper clips, 2 washers" is better than "a few."

5

Redesign: Look at your Trial 1 data. What's one thing you could change to pick up more objects? Make that single change and record it in your "design changes" column. Then test again and record Trial 2.

6

One More Improvement: Make a second design change for Trial 3. After all three trials, circle your best result. What made it work better than the others?

🌲
Think Like an Engineer
"When you made a change to your design, what did your data actually show? Did the result go up, down, or stay the same — and what does that tell you about what matters?"
4
📓 Record Your Thinking
Organize your data and explain what you found.
⏱ 8 min

A data table makes it easy to spot patterns. In your notebook, set up a table with three columns: Trial | Objects Collected | Design Change. Fill it in for all three rounds.

1 Which trial collected the most objects? What was different about that design?
2 What pattern do you notice across your three trials? Did results generally get better, worse, or stay about the same?
3 Draw and label your best prototype design. Label at least 4 parts: the handle, the attachment point, the string, and the magnet.
🗣

Tell the story of your engineering challenge — what you built first, what you changed, and what the data showed you. Use the numbers from your table to explain your thinking.

1. You hold a magnet near a pile of paper clips. The clips jump toward the magnet without being touched. What is causing this?
A
Gravity pulling the clips upward
B
Magnetic force acting at a distance
C
Friction between the clips and the table
⭐ Exactly right! Magnetic force works even when the magnet and the object aren't touching — that's what makes it so useful for engineering tools.
Think about what you know about magnets. Do the magnet and the paper clip need to be touching for the clip to move?
2. You test your magnetic rescue tool and it only picks up 2 paper clips. What should you do next?
A
Start over with a completely new design from scratch
B
Keep testing with the same design — never change anything
C
Make one change, test again, and compare the results
⭐ That's the engineering way! Changing one thing at a time gives you clear evidence of what's actually making the difference.
Engineers don't scrap everything or refuse to change anything. They test, look at the data, and make small, thoughtful improvements.

⭐ Yes! Without data, you're just guessing. With numbers from each trial, you can see exactly which change improved the result — and which didn't.
Data isn't just a rule. Think about how it helped you during the challenge today. What would have been harder without the numbers?
