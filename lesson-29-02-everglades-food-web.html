Section 3
Every Organism Plays a Role

In a food web, every organism matters. If one disappears, the animals that ate it can go hungry — and the animals it used to eat can grow out of control. Scientists call this a cascade: one change triggers another, and another, rippling through the whole web.

Real-World Connection: Scientists studying the Everglades found that when alligator holes dry up during droughts, fish crowd into smaller pools and become easy prey. Snail kites, anhingas, and raccoons all shift where they hunt — one weather event can ripple through the entire food web in a single season.

⭐ Key Concept

In a food web, every organism is connected. Energy flows from producers up through consumers to apex predators, with decomposers returning nutrients at every level.

Because every organism is connected, removing even one species can cause changes — called a cascade — that ripple through the whole system.

🤯
Amazing Everglades Fact
The snail kite eats almost nothing but apple snails. When a drought hit parts of the Everglades in 2007, apple snail populations crashed — and snail kite chicks starved in the nest that same season. One drought. One food source. One season. The whole population felt it.
Curious? Go Deeper

Scientists have been working to restore the Everglades for decades. Over a century of drainage canals reduced it to about half its original size — and with less water, the food web changed dramatically. Fewer fish meant fewer anhingas and wading birds. Fewer birds meant fewer alligators nesting near water.

The Comprehensive Everglades Restoration Plan, started in 2000, is one of the largest ecosystem restoration projects ever attempted. Engineers are redirecting water to refill the marsh. When water levels rise, periphyton grows back first. Then mosquitofish return. Then the wading birds. The food web rebuilds itself — from the bottom up.

Think about that: restoring one thing (water flow) can restart an entire food web. What does that tell you about how connected all these organisms really are?

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you remember before starting the notebook work.
1. In a food web diagram, what does an arrow show?
AThe path energy takes — from the organism being eaten to the organism eating it
BWhich organism is bigger
CHow fast an animal can move
🌿 Exactly right! The arrow follows the energy — it points from prey to predator, showing which direction energy flows through the web.
Think about it this way: in a food web, something is always getting eaten and something is doing the eating. The arrow shows where the energy goes — from the eaten to the eater. Try again!
2. Sawgrass is a producer. What does that mean?
AIt eats fish and small animals
BIt makes its own food using energy from sunlight
CIt breaks down dead organisms in the marsh
🌱 That's it! Producers are at the base of every food web — they're the only organisms that can capture energy from the sun and turn it into food.
Remember: producers don't eat — they make their own food. Think about what plants do with sunlight. Which answer fits that idea?
3. Apple snails disappeared from part of the Everglades. What would most likely happen to the snail kite population?
ASnail kites would struggle to survive — apple snails are almost their only food
BSnail kites would do fine — they would just eat more sawgrass
CNothing would change — snail kites don't need food
⭐ Right! The snail kite is a specialist — it depends on almost one food source. When apple snails are gone, snail kites go hungry. That's exactly what happened in 2007 during a drought in the Everglades.
Think back to the organism table. What does the snail kite eat? It's very specific — that's a big clue. Which answer matches what you'd expect for an animal with only one food source?
