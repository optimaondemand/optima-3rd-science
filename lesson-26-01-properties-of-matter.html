📖 The Lesson: Properties of Matter
Read and think carefully — science is all about noticing!
⏱ 12 min
🌱
Section 1
What Is Matter?

Everything around you is made of matterMatterAnything that takes up space and has weight.. Your desk, the air you breathe, even the water you drink — it is all matter.

Matter is anything that takes up space and has weight. Scientists study matter by looking closely at its propertiesPropertyA feature you can observe or measure..

🤔
Stop and Think
"Can you think of something that is NOT matter? What about light or sound — do they take up space?"
🌤
Section 2
Observable Properties

Every object has properties you can see, feel, or measure. Some are easy to spot — color, shape, and size. Others take more careful looking.

TextureTextureHow the surface feels — smooth, rough, bumpy. tells you how the surface feels. HardnessHardnessHow easily something can be scratched. tells you if it scratches easily or not. A rock is hard. Clay is soft.

🌍
Section 3
Measuring Properties

Some properties need tools to measure. Scientists use a balance to find massMassHow much matter is in an object.. They use rulers and containers to find volumeVolumeHow much space an object takes up..

Real-World Connection: When you pick up a bowling ball and then pick up a basketball, the bowling ball feels much heavier — it has more mass.

⭐ Key Concept

Every object is made of matter and has properties that scientists can observe and measure, like mass, volume, color, texture, and hardness.

🤯
Amazing Nature Fact
A teaspoon of a neutron star would weigh about 6 billion tons — that is more mass than every car on Earth combined, squeezed into one tiny spoon!
Curious? Sorting by Properties

Scientists sort objects into groups based on their properties. You could sort rocks by color, or by hardness, or by texture. Each way of sorting tells you something different.

When you sort objects into groups, you are classifying them. Classification helps scientists organize the world and find patterns.

Next time you see a pile of objects, try sorting them two different ways. What do you notice?

Now test the idea yourself.

3
🔬 Properties Sort
Observe, describe, and compare objects by their properties
⏱ 15–20 min
🎒

You'll need: 3–5 small objects from around your home (a coin, eraser, spoon, rock, sponge, etc.)

Follow each step carefully. Record your observations in your science notebook as you go.

1

🔎 Step 1: Gather and Observe: Find 3–5 small objects. Look at each one carefully. What color is it? What shape? How does the surface feel?

2

📋 Step 2: Record Properties: Copy the properties chart below into your notebook. Fill in each column for every object.

3

⚖️ Step 3: Compare: Hold two objects in your hands. Which one feels heavier? Which is smoother? Write your comparisons in your notebook.

4

📝 Step 4: Classify: Sort your objects into two groups based on one property (for example: rough vs. smooth). Then re-sort them a different way (heavy vs. light).

🌲
Think Like a Scientist
"Which property was easiest to observe? Which one was hardest? Why?"

BThe place where an object is found
CHow fast an object moves

Not quite. Think about what you can see, feel, or measure about an object — that is a property.

AColor
BMass
CShape
🌲 Yes! The rock has more mass than the sponge — that is why it feels heavier.
Think again. When something feels heavier, it has more of which property? Hint: it starts with M.
3. Which tool would a scientist use to measure the mass of an object?
AA ruler
BA thermometer
CA balance or scale
🌲 Exactly! A balance or scale measures mass — how much matter is in an object.
A ruler measures length and a thermometer measures temperature. Which tool measures how heavy something is?
