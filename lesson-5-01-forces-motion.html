📖 The Lesson: Forces & Motion
Read and think carefully — science is all about noticing!

💥
Section 1
What Is a Force?

A forceforceA push or pull that acts on an object and can change its motion. is any push or pull that acts on an object. Forces can change how fast something moves, which direction it goes, or whether it moves at all.

Every force has two things: strength (how hard the push or pull is) and direction (which way it's pushing or pulling). A gentle tap on a ball is a small force. A hard kick is a much bigger force — and the ball moves further and faster.

🤔
Stop and Think
"If you push a box lightly, it barely moves. If you push it hard, it slides far. What changed — the object or the force?"
⚖️
Section 2
Balanced and Unbalanced Forces

Balanced forcesbalanced forcesEqual forces from opposite directions that cancel each other out — no change in motion. happen when two equal forces push or pull from opposite directions. Think of a tug-of-war where both teams pull exactly the same — the rope doesn't move. The forces cancel out.

Section 3
Changing How Objects Move

Not all forces are the same size. A gentle tap on a soccer ball sends it rolling slowly. A hard kick sends it flying fast. The speedspeedHow fast an object moves. A stronger force usually creates more speed. of an object changes when the strength of the force changes.

The directiondirectionThe path an object travels. A force from the left sends an object right — and vice versa. of a force matters too. Push a ball straight ahead and it goes forward. Push it from the side and it curves. Scientists study exactly these patterns — how changing a force changes what happens.

Real-World Connection: Look at the photo above. The moment that foot connects with the ball, an unbalanced force acts on it — the ball speeds up and changes direction all at once.

⭐ Key Concept

Forces cause objects to start moving, stop moving, speed up, slow down, or change direction. When forces are balanced, nothing changes. When forces are unbalanced, motion changes.

The strength and direction of a force both matter — changing either one changes what happens to the object.

🤯
Amazing Forces Fact
A professional soccer player can kick a ball at over 80 miles per hour — fast enough to travel the length of a school bus in less than one second. That's a single unbalanced force changing an object's speed almost instantly.
Curious? Go Deeper

Why don't things keep moving forever once you push them? On Earth, another force is almost always present: friction. Friction is a force that acts against motion whenever two surfaces touch. When you push a ball across the floor, friction between the ball and the carpet slows it down. Engineers think hard about friction — sometimes they want it (brakes on a car), and sometimes they want to reduce it (wheels on a wagon).

In outer space, there's no air and almost no friction. If an astronaut pushes off a wall, they keep floating in that direction until another force acts on them. The International Space Station has been falling toward Earth for over 25 years — but it's also moving sideways so fast that it keeps "missing" the planet. That continuous fall is what creates weightlessness!

🔬 Investigation: Push vs. Pull
Test how force strength and direction change motion
⏱ 20 min
🎒

You'll need: a small object (toy car, ball, or eraser), a flat surface, a ruler, and your science notebook.

You're going to test how forces change motion. You'll push and pull the same object in different ways and record exactly what you observe each time.

📷 Photo · What Your Investigation Might Look Like
🖼️
Suggested Image — Generate This
Close-up of a child's hands on a smooth hardwood classroom floor pushing a small toy car or wooden block forward with one finger. A ruler is placed alongside to measure the distance traveled. Slightly elevated angle showing the hands, object, and ruler clearly. Warm natural window light. Real photograph, no white background, no cartoons.
Save as: images/5-01-force-investigation.png
One object, a flat surface, and a ruler to measure — that's all you need. Push gently, then push hard, and record what changes each time.
1

Make a prediction. In your notebook, write what you think will happen: will a strong push move the object farther than a gentle push? Will pulling from a different direction change where it goes? Write your best guess before you test.

2

Test gentle vs. strong. Push your object gently and use your ruler to measure how far it moved. Then push it hard from the same starting spot. Record both distances in your notebook.

3

Change the direction. Now push your object from a different angle — from the left side, then the right. Observe which way it moves each time. Draw an arrow in your notebook showing each force direction and where the object went.

4

Compare to your prediction. Look at what you recorded. Did the results match what you expected? What surprised you? Write one sentence explaining what the data tells you about how forces change motion.

🧠
Think Like an Engineer
"You changed the strength of the force and the direction of the force. Which one changed the motion more — and how do you know from your data?"

📓 Record Your Thinking
Pause and think about what you built, tested, and figured out.
⏱ 8 min

Think about it: As you look back at your investigation, consider these questions. You'll use your best thinking in the Write & Explain section.

1 Create a data table with three columns: Type of Force (push or pull), Strength (gentle or strong), Distance Moved. Fill in a row for each test you did.
2 What pattern do you notice in your data? What happened to the distance when the force got stronger? Use the words "force" and "motion" in your answer.
3 Draw a top-view diagram of your object. Draw two arrows showing: (1) a gentle push and where the object went, (2) a strong push and where it went. Label each arrow.
🗣

Tell the story of your investigation from beginning to end — what you tested, what happened when you changed the force, and what you figured out from your results.
