# 3rd Grade Science Teacher Grading Guide

Use this guide with the Assignment tab in each lesson HTML file. Answers are suggested, not scripted. For open-ended prompts, drawings, models, and student-data tasks, grade for accurate science ideas, complete sentences when requested, and evidence from the student's own work.

When a prompt depends on a student's investigation data, the guide says "answers vary." In those cases, check that the student uses their actual data, names the pattern correctly, and explains why the evidence supports the answer.

## Lesson 1.01: How Do Scientists Observe the World?

- Part A 1: Inference.
- Part A 2: Data.
- Part A 3: Observation.
- Part A 4: Evidence.
- Part B Write and Explain: A strong answer says scientists observe carefully before explaining so their ideas are based on evidence, not guesses. It should connect observation to inference and mention that careful details help scientists avoid mistakes.
- Part C Draw and Label: Look for an outdoor field scientist scene, at least two labeled tools, a labeled object or event being observed, and one arrow or note showing an inference based on evidence.
- Reflection: Accept a thoughtful sentence about how the student now sees scientists as careful observers who collect evidence.

## Lesson 1.02: Investigation: Becoming a Scientist

- Part A Match the Sense: Labels should match each observation: sight for things seen, sound for things heard, smell for scents, and touch for texture, temperature, or shape felt by hand. The "most useful sense" answer can vary but should include a clear reason from the investigation.
- Part B Write and Explain: A strong response names a specific observation, explains what it suggests, and connects the inference to evidence. Example: "I observed that the object was smooth, cold, and heavy. That tells me it might be a rock because those details are evidence from my senses."
- Part C Scientific Sketch: Look for at least three labeled parts, careful details, and labels tied to observations rather than guesses.
- Reflection: Accept a personal but specific comment about how doing an investigation changed the student's idea of a scientist.

## Lesson 2.01: What Patterns Do We See in Weather?

- Part A Identify and Label: The five weather symbols should be correctly labeled based on the lesson diagram. Examples should be specific, such as sunny in the afternoon, cloudy before rain, windy during a storm front, rainy during a wet day, or snowy in winter if snow is one of the symbols.
- Part B Write and Explain: A strong answer describes temperature, clouds, precipitation, and wind; names a likely weather pattern; and uses one observation as evidence for the prediction.
- Part C Draw and Label: Look for a sky sketch with at least four labels, including clouds or clear sky, sun, precipitation if present, and temperature. The sentence should describe the observed weather.
- Reflection: Accept an update to the student's original prediction that uses new weather evidence.

## Lesson 2.02: Investigation: Graphing Weekly Weather

- Part A 1: Answers vary by the student's graph. Check that the student identifies the highest-temperature day and gives the correct temperature from the graph.
- Part A 2: Answers vary by the student's graph. Check that the student identifies the lowest-temperature day and subtracts lowest from highest correctly.
- Part A 3: Answers vary by the student's weather data. Check that the student identifies the rainy day and correctly compares it with the previous day.
- Part A 4: Answers vary. A strong pattern statement describes whether temperatures rose, fell, stayed steady, or changed up and down. The Saturday prediction should include a number and evidence from the graph.

- Reflection: Accept a comparison of list versus graph that explains why one made patterns easier to see.

## Lesson 3.01: Why Are National Parks So Different?

- Part A 1: Chart includes Desert, Wetland, and Mountain, with temperature, precipitation, and at least two plants or animals for each.
- Part A 2: Chart is color-coded with at least two colors, including one for most precipitation and one for least precipitation.
- Part A 3: Death Valley = Desert; Everglades = Wetland; Rocky Mountain National Park = Mountain.
- Part B 4: Weather is what the air is like at a certain time or day; climate is the usual weather pattern over many years. Example: "It is raining today" is weather; "This park is usually dry" is climate.
- Part B 5: More sunlight generally makes a place warmer and can change the climate, which can affect precipitation, plants, animals, and survival.
- Part B 6: A wetland has much more water and precipitation than a desert. Wetlands support water-loving plants and animals; deserts have dry conditions and organisms adapted to little water.
- Write and Explain: A strong answer compares two very different zones, uses chart evidence, and explains how sunlight, precipitation, or elevation helps cause the difference.
- Part C Draw and Label: Look for two side-by-side climate zones, one dry and one wet, each with typical temperature, one plant, and one animal labeled.

## Lesson 5.01: How Do Forces Change Motion?

- Part A 1: Balanced forces. The dog pulls one way and the owner pulls back; because the dog stays in place, the forces are balanced.
- Part A 2: The kick is a push force; friction and air resistance slow the ball. The forces are unbalanced when motion changes.
- Part A 3: Balanced forces; equal pushes from opposite sides cancel, so the box does not move.
- Part B 4: A force is a push or pull. Examples should include one push and one pull from everyday life.
- Part B 5: Balanced forces do not change an object's motion; unbalanced forces change speed, direction, or start/stop motion.
- Part B 6: In the soccer photo, acceptable forces include the foot pushing the ball, gravity pulling the ball down, and friction or air slowing it. At the kick, forces are unbalanced because the ball changes motion.
- Write and Explain: A strong answer says a harder push makes the book move faster or farther because a stronger force causes a greater change in motion.
- Part C 7: Balanced-force diagram should show equal arrows in opposite directions and label the object and force directions.
- Part C 8: Unbalanced-force soccer diagram should show a force arrow in the kick direction and identify the forces as unbalanced.

## Lesson 9.01: How Do Animals Grow and Change?

- Part A 1: Egg, tadpole, froglet, adult frog.
- Part A 2: Arrows should show the cycle moving from egg to tadpole to froglet to adult frog and back to eggs.
- Part A 3: Most dramatic change is usually tadpole to froglet/adult frog because the body grows legs, loses tail, and changes breathing.

## Lesson 9.02: Investigation: Compare Animal Life Cycles
