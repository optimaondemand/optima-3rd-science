🌾
Section 1
A River of Grass

The EvergladesEvergladesA vast wetland covering much of South Florida — the largest subtropical wilderness in the United States. isn't a lake or a pond. Scientists call it a "river of grass." Water moves slowly south through millions of acres of sawgrassSawgrassA tall, razor-edged plant that grows in thick mats across the Everglades' shallow water. — just a few inches deep, barely moving, stretching as far as you can see.

The ground is almost always wet, and the land is almost perfectly flat. That's what makes it a wetlandWetlandA place where water covers or soaks the soil much of the year. Unique plants and animals live here. — a place shaped entirely by water.

🤔
Stop and Think
"What kinds of animals do you think could survive in a place that is always flat, warm, and wet?"
🦎
Section 2
Who Fits In Here?

The Everglades is home to animals adaptedAdaptedHaving features that help an organism survive in its particular home, like webbed feet for swimming. for warm, wet conditions. American alligators wait in shallow pools. Great blue herons strike at fish. Roseate spoonbills sweep their bills through the water. These animals fit the habitatHabitatThe specific place where an organism lives and finds food, water, and shelter to survive. like a key in a lock.

But not every animal fits. A bear needing dry forest, or a polar bear needing ice — they'd struggle here. The ecosystemEcosystemAll the living things in an area and the water, air, and soil they all depend on. decides who stays and who can't.

🌊
Section 3
Water, Flooding, and Protection

When big storms hit South Florida, enormous amounts of rain fall fast. The Everglades soaks it up — the land absorbs water like a giant sponge. That slows flooding and protects nearby farms and cities. Scientists and engineers work to protect the Everglades' natural water flowWater FlowThe slow movement of water across flat land, like a shallow river hidden in a field of grass. because restoring it is one of the best ways to reduce flood damage.

Real-World Connection: The Everglades is home to the largest wetland restoration project ever attempted — engineers rebuilding old water routes so nature can do what it does best.

Five major zones in the Everglades ecosystem — each one supports different organisms and plays a different role.
⭐ Key Concept

Whether an organism survives in the Everglades depends on how well it fits the warm, wet, flat habitat. Animals that need dry ground or cold temperatures will struggle — while those adapted to shallow water can thrive.

The same water that supports wildlife also acts as a natural sponge — slowing floods and protecting nearby communities.

🤯
Amazing Nature Fact
The Everglades is the only place on Earth where alligators and crocodiles naturally live side by side. Alligators prefer freshwater inland; crocodiles prefer the salty coastal edges — and the Everglades has both.
Curious? Go Deeper

What happened to the Everglades? About 100 years ago, people built hundreds of miles of canals through the Everglades to drain water away for farms and cities. The ecosystem shrank — less water moving meant fewer fish, fewer birds, and fewer small animals that everything else eats.

Now engineers are working on the largest wetland restoration project ever attempted — billions of dollars rebuilding the original water routes so the water can flow the way it used to. It's slow work, but birds are already returning to areas where they hadn't been seen in decades.

Here's the question scientists are still working on: how do you restore a living system damaged for over 100 years, while 8 million people still live right next to it?

You'll need: Science notebook and pencil. Read the animal cards below, then copy and fill in the chart in your notebook.

🐊 American Alligator

Cold-blooded reptile. Needs warm water. Hunts fish and turtles in shallow freshwater. Thrives in slow, warm, flat water.

🐦 Great Blue Heron

Large wading bird with long legs. Hunts by standing still and striking fast at fish. Needs warm, shallow water with fish nearby.

🐻 Florida Black Bear

Omnivore that eats berries, insects, and small animals. Needs dry forest to travel, den, and raise cubs.

🐻‍❄️ Polar Bear

Built for Arctic survival. Needs sea ice to hunt seals. Thick white fur for extreme cold. Body overheats above 50°F.

🦩 Roseate Spoonbill

Bright pink wading bird. Sweeps its spoon-shaped bill through shallow warm water to catch tiny fish. Nests in mangroves near water.

🧠 Quick Self-Check
Not graded — just for you! See how much you remember.
1. What do scientists call the Everglades because water moves slowly through a vast field of grass?
AA frozen tundra
BA river of grass
CA saltwater marsh
🌿 That's right! The Everglades is called a "river of grass" — water flows slowly south through miles of sawgrass, just inches deep.
Think about how water moves through the Everglades — slowly south through thick sawgrass. Scientists gave this a special name describing what they saw.
2. An animal that needs dry land, cool temperatures, and thick forest would most likely _____ in the Everglades.
AThrive and become the top predator
BAdapt quickly and do fine
CStruggle to survive because the habitat doesn't match its needs
🌿 Exactly! The Everglades is warm, flat, and wet — an animal that needs dry, cool, forested land won't find a matching habitat here.
Think about what the Everglades is like: warm, wet, and mostly grass and water. Does that match what this animal needs?
3. How does the Everglades help protect nearby communities when big storms bring heavy rain?
AIt absorbs water like a giant sponge, slowing down flooding
BIt creates strong winds that push storms away from cities
CIt freezes the rain before it reaches the ground
⭐ Yes! The Everglades soaks up enormous amounts of rainwater, slowing floods and protecting farms and cities nearby.
Remember — the Everglades acts a lot like a sponge. What does a sponge do when you pour water on it?
